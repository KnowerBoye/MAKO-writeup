\chapter{LITERATURE REVIEW} % Main chapter title


The evolution of Large Language Models (LLMs) has transcended the boundaries of simple linguistic synthesis, moving toward the realization of agentic systems capable of autonomous, multi-step task execution \citep{Jian_2025}. While the generative prowess of these models is undeniable, their deployment as singular autonomous entities has exposed significant intrinsic limitations, most notably the propensity for hallucination and the constraints of their autoregressive nature \citep{tran2025multiagentcollaborationmechanismssurvey}. These models often struggle with "slow-thinking" processes \citep{hagendorff2023human} the systematic, deliberative reasoning required for complex problem-solving and face diminishing returns as scaling laws meet the reality of open-ended computational logic \citep{kaplan2020scaling}. To bridge these gaps, most implementations of agentic applications have pivoted toward Multi-Agent Systems (MAS), under the observation that a collective of specialized agents consistently outperforms individual models by distributing cognitive load and introducing cross-verification \citep{albada2025chapter}. This shift, however, necessitates a rigorous definition of how these agents collaborate, communicate, and are orchestrated to ensure that the collective output remains coherent and goal-oriented.

\citet{tran2025multiagentcollaborationmechanismssurvey} describes orchestration in the context of MAS as a multifaceted discipline encompassing collaboration types, coordination strategies, and specific communication structures designed to manage the flow of information between disparate agents. A primary focus within this field is the "sequencing" of agent interventions, which dictates the operational logic of the workflow. In many current frameworks, this sequencing is either statically defined through manual scripting or relies on heuristic-based "context hand-offs," where the state of a task is passed linearly from one agent to the next based on probabilistic triggers. 
Such strategies, while functional for experimental or simple workflows, frequently fail to meet the reliability benchmarks required of enterprise systems, where reliability is defined by the consistency and accuracy of generated outputs \citep{menshawy2025llms}.
Because enterprise deployments demand that reliability be maintained through rigorous testing, validation phases, and robust version control, the inherent "stochastic guessing" of probabilistic sequencing introduces unacceptable inaccuracies. The reliance on models that may be compromised by training biases or learning anomalies necessitates a move toward orchestration that does not just "predict" the next step, but validates it against a quality assurance framework ensuring that every agentic transition is as dependable as a traditional enterprise software deployment.

To anchor these agentic decisions in reality, researchers have historically looked to Knowledge Graphs (KGs) as a robust mechanism for grounding LLM outputs \citep{kau2024combiningknowledgegraphslarge}. Knowledge Graphs provide a structured, symbolic representation of a domain, offering a "Source of Truth" and a relational depth that probabilistic models inherently lack \citep{Hogan_2021}. By integrating KGs into the decision-making loop, systems can verify agent claims against a persistent, factual schema, thereby mitigating the risks of stochastic drift and ensuring semantic consistency. The intersection of graph-based reasoning and agent orchestration represents a significant frontier in AI research, as it promises to transform the way agents understand their environment and their own functional capabilities. This historical precedent for KG-driven grounding suggests that the next logical step in the evolution of MAS is the use of graph structures not just for static data retrieval, but as the fundamental logic gate for orchestration itself.

This chapter provides a comprehensive examination of existing approaches to multi-agent orchestration, tracing the trajectory from general collaboration frameworks to specialized strategies focused on agent sequencing and context hand-off, as well as emerging approaches that propose dynamic methods for managing these transitions. By critically analyzing these methodologies, the review highlights a persistent limitation: the lack of a truly dynamic and deterministic way to decide the order of execution in complex, non-linear workflows. Many current "dynamic" models still operate within the vacuum of the LLM’s context window, leaving the sequencing vulnerable to the same autoregressive failures the MAS architecture was intended to solve. Through this analysis, we establish the critical need for a fact-grounded approach to dynamic agent sequencing. This leads directly to the core contribution of this project: a Knowledge Graph-Based Multi-Agent Orchestration Framework that utilizes a deterministic world model to ensure that every agentic transition is logically validated and anchored in the objective reality of the graph.

\section{Introduction to Concepts}
This section provides comprehensive explanations of the key concepts from LLM-Based Agents, Multi-Agent Systems to Knowledge Graphs, ensuring a solid theoretical foundation.

\subsection{LLM-Based Agents}
To define the foundational components of the framework, it is first necessary to establish the architectural anatomy of an LLM-Based Agent. Unlike a standard language model, which functions as a passive text generator, an agent is a semi-autonomous system that uses the LLM as its "reasoning engine" to interact with an external environment. The literature \citet{xi2023risepotentiallargelanguage} generally decomposes an agent into three primary pillars Memory, Planning, and Tool Use often abstracted as the "brain," "perception," and "action" of the system.

Memory allows the agent to maintain state over time, categorizing information into short-term storage (the context window) and long-term storage (external databases or Retrieval-Augmented Generation) \citep{albada2025chapterMemory}. 

Tool use \citep{schick2023toolformerlanguagemodelsteach,parisi2022talmtoolaugmentedlanguage} enables the agent to transcend the "digital silo" of its training data by invoking external APIs, executing code, or querying databases to retrieve real-time information. 

However, the most critical and most volatile component is the Planning layer, which governs how the agent cognitively breaks down a complex objective into executable steps.
The current state-of-the-art in agentic planning relies almost exclusively on prompt-based techniques designed to elicit "System 2" thinking deliberative, analytical reasoning from the model. Strategies such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) force the model to generate intermediate reasoning steps \citep{wei2023chainofthoughtpromptingelicitsreasoning}, while Tree-of-Thoughts (ToT) allows the agent to explore multiple reasoning branches and self-evaluate their progress in a non-linear fashion \citep{yao2023treethoughtsdeliberateproblem}. More advanced frameworks like ReAct (Reasoning and Acting) create a feedback loop where the model interleaves thoughts, actions, and observations to refine its trajectory \citep{yao2023reactsynergizingreasoningacting}. 

While these methods significantly improve performance on isolated tasks, they remain purely probabilistic and heuristic. The planning is "soft-coded" within the prompt, meaning the agent's logic is only as reliable as the model's next-token prediction. There are no hard constraints to prevent the agent from skipping a vital step or proposing a logically impossible action, as the "plan" is essentially a sophisticated guess based on linguistic patterns rather than a validated structural map.

In terms of Task Execution, LLM-based agents typically operate through either direct handling or tool invocation. 
In the former, the LLM processes information and generates a response based on its internal weights; in the latter, the agent must translate its intent into a structured output \citep{Liu_2024}, such as a JSON-formatted function call, to interact with an execution tool. 
The tool returns a raw response, which the LLM must then re-process into a natural language summary or use as a catalyst for the next step in the plan. 
This execution phase is where the "grounding" of the agent is tested, as it requires the model to precisely map its internal reasoning to the strict syntactic requirements of external software. 
Any misalignment between the LLM's generative output and the tool's expected schema results in execution failure, highlighting the fragility of relying on linguistic intuition for technical interoperability.



Despite the sophistication of these components, single-agent systems frequently succumb to catastrophic failure when applied to complex, multi-stage enterprise workflows. These failures are primarily attributed to the "unconstrained" nature of prompt-based planning \citep{rana2025modelfirstreasoningllmagents}. Without an external governing structure, a single agent often enters a hallucination recursive loop, where it attempts to correct an initial error by generating further inaccurate logic based on that false premise \citep{latitude2024taskcomplexity,cheng2025chainofthoughtpromptingobscureshallucination}. Additionally, as the task complexity increases, the agent suffers from contextual overload; the accumulation of previous thoughts, tool outputs, and observations clutters the context window, leading to a precipitous degradation in reasoning quality \citep{anthropic2024context}. This architectural fragility underscores the necessity for moving away from isolated, prompt-driven agents toward a more rigid, orchestrated multi-agent environment where the "world model" is held by a Knowledge Graph rather than the volatile and finite memory of a single model.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Systems}
The evolution of agentic architectures from single-model entities to Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) represents a strategic transition toward functional decomposition and collaborative problem-solving. 
In a MAS environment, a complex global objective is partitioned into discrete sub-tasks, each managed by specialized agents possessing distinct roles, personas, and toolsets \citep{yang2024llmbasedmultiagentsystemstechniques}. 
This shift is predicated on the observation that the collective intelligence of a modular system consistently outperforms the reasoning capacity of a monolithic LLM, particularly in tasks requiring high cognitive diversity or parallel execution \citep{li2024more_agents,guo2024largelanguagemodelbased}. By isolating specific domains of expertise such as an agent dedicated solely to code generation and another to quality assurance the system introduces a layer of intersubjective verifiability, where agents can critique and refine each other's outputs, effectively serving as a circuit breaker for the "hallucination recursive loops" prevalent in single-model configurations.

Despite these advantages, the transition to MAS introduces a significant coordination overhead, commonly referred to as the “Coordination Tax” \citep{kim2025sciencescalingagentsystems}, as the number of agents increases, the complexity of managing their interactions grows exponentially, necessitating sophisticated communication protocols and collaboration strategies. 
\citet{wang2025largemodelbasedagents} distinguishes between "Horizontal" collaboration, where agents interact as peers in an unstructured manner, and "Vertical" or "Hierarchical" orchestration, where a lead agent or central controller dictates the workflow. Without a formal governing structure, even these collaborative models remain bound by the limitations of the underlying models' ability to maintain a consistent state across fragmented context windows.

Consequently, \citet{valmeekam2023planbenchextensiblebenchmarkevaluating} underscores that while Large Language Models (LLMs) can generate text that mimics the structure of a plan, they consistently fail at logically validated execution. This empirical "planning gap" demonstrates that agent collaboration and orchestration cannot be left to the probabilistic nature of the LLM alone. By shifting the responsibility of workflow logic from the LLM to a structured world model, the framework ensures that every agentic action is not just linguistically plausible, but mathematically verified and fully auditable. This transition effectively moves the system from a paradigm of "stochastic sequencing" to one of "deterministic coordination," providing the reliability required for enterprise-grade applications.
