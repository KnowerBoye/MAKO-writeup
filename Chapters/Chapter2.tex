\section{Introduction}

The evolution of Large Language Models (LLMs) has transcended the boundaries of simple linguistic synthesis, moving toward the realization of agentic systems capable of autonomous, multi-step task execution \citep{Jian_2025}. While the generative prowess of these models is undeniable, their deployment as singular autonomous entities has exposed significant intrinsic limitations, most notably the propensity for hallucination and the constraints of their autoregressive nature \citep{tran2025multiagentcollaborationmechanismssurvey}. These models often struggle with "slow-thinking" processes \citep{hagendorff2023human} the systematic, deliberative reasoning required for complex problem-solving and face diminishing returns as scaling laws meet the reality of open-ended computational logic \citep{kaplan2020scaling}. To bridge these gaps, most implementations of agentic applications have pivoted toward Multi-Agent Systems (MAS), under the observation that a collective of specialized agents consistently outperforms individual models by distributing cognitive load and introducing cross-verification \citep{albada2025chapter}. This shift, however, necessitates a rigorous definition of how these agents collaborate, communicate, and are orchestrated to ensure that the collective output remains coherent and goal-oriented.

\citet{tran2025multiagentcollaborationmechanismssurvey} describes orchestration in the context of MAS as a multifaceted discipline encompassing collaboration types, coordination strategies, and specific communication structures designed to manage the flow of information between disparate agents. A primary focus within this field is the "sequencing" of agent interventions, which dictates the operational logic of the workflow. In many current frameworks, this sequencing is either statically defined through manual scripting or relies on heuristic-based "context hand-offs," where the state of a task is passed linearly from one agent to the next based on probabilistic triggers. 
Such strategies, while functional for experimental or simple workflows, frequently fail to meet the reliability benchmarks required of enterprise systems, where reliability is defined by the consistency and accuracy of generated outputs \citep{menshawy2025llms}.
Because enterprise deployments demand that reliability be maintained through rigorous testing, validation phases, and robust version control, the inherent "stochastic guessing" of probabilistic sequencing introduces unacceptable inaccuracies. The reliance on models that may be compromised by training biases or learning anomalies necessitates a move toward orchestration that does not just "predict" the next step, but validates it against a quality assurance framework ensuring that every agentic transition is as dependable as a traditional enterprise software deployment.

To anchor these agentic decisions in reality, researchers have historically looked to Knowledge Graphs (KGs) as a robust mechanism for grounding LLM outputs \citep{kau2024combiningknowledgegraphslarge}. Knowledge Graphs provide a structured, symbolic representation of a domain, offering a "Source of Truth" and a relational depth that probabilistic models inherently lack \citep{Hogan_2021}. By integrating KGs into the decision-making loop, systems can verify agent claims against a persistent, factual schema, thereby mitigating the risks of stochastic drift and ensuring semantic consistency. The intersection of graph-based reasoning and agent orchestration represents a significant frontier in AI research, as it promises to transform the way agents understand their environment and their own functional capabilities. This historical precedent for KG-driven grounding suggests that the next logical step in the evolution of MAS is the use of graph structures not just for static data retrieval, but as the fundamental logic gate for orchestration itself.

This chapter provides a comprehensive examination of existing approaches to multi-agent orchestration, tracing the trajectory from general collaboration frameworks to specialized strategies focused on agent sequencing and context hand-off, as well as emerging approaches that propose dynamic methods for managing these transitions. By critically analyzing these methodologies, the review highlights a persistent limitation: the lack of a truly dynamic and deterministic way to decide the order of execution in complex, non-linear workflows. Many current "dynamic" models still operate within the vacuum of the LLM’s context window, leaving the sequencing vulnerable to the same autoregressive failures the MAS architecture was intended to solve. Through this analysis, we establish the critical need for a fact-grounded approach to dynamic agent sequencing. This leads directly to the core contribution of this project: a Knowledge Graph-Based Multi-Agent Orchestration Framework that utilizes a deterministic world model to ensure that every agentic transition is logically validated and anchored in the objective reality of the graph.

\section{Introduction to Concepts}
This section provides comprehensive explanations of the key concepts from LLM-Based Agents, Multi-Agent Systems to Knowledge Graphs, ensuring a solid theoretical foundation.

\subsection{LLM-Based Agents}
To define the foundational components of the framework, it is first necessary to establish the architectural anatomy of an LLM-Based Agent. Unlike a standard language model, which functions as a passive text generator, an agent is a semi-autonomous system that uses the LLM as its "reasoning engine" to interact with an external environment. The literature \citet{xi2023risepotentiallargelanguage} generally decomposes an agent into three primary pillars Memory, Planning, and Tool Use often abstracted as the "brain," "perception," and "action" of the system.

Memory allows the agent to maintain state over time, categorizing information into short-term storage (the context window) and long-term storage (external databases or Retrieval-Augmented Generation) \citep{albada2025chapterMemory}. 

Tool use \citep{schick2023toolformerlanguagemodelsteach,parisi2022talmtoolaugmentedlanguage} enables the agent to transcend the "digital silo" of its training data by invoking external APIs, executing code, or querying databases to retrieve real-time information. 

However, the most critical and most volatile component is the Planning layer, which governs how the agent cognitively breaks down a complex objective into executable steps.
The current state-of-the-art in agentic planning relies almost exclusively on prompt-based techniques designed to elicit "System 2" thinking deliberative, analytical reasoning from the model. Strategies such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) force the model to generate intermediate reasoning steps \citep{wei2023chainofthoughtpromptingelicitsreasoning}, while Tree-of-Thoughts (ToT) allows the agent to explore multiple reasoning branches and self-evaluate their progress in a non-linear fashion \citep{yao2023treethoughtsdeliberateproblem}. More advanced frameworks like ReAct (Reasoning and Acting) create a feedback loop where the model interleaves thoughts, actions, and observations to refine its trajectory \citep{yao2023reactsynergizingreasoningacting}. 

While these methods significantly improve performance on isolated tasks, they remain purely probabilistic and heuristic. The planning is "soft-coded" within the prompt, meaning the agent's logic is only as reliable as the model's next-token prediction. There are no hard constraints to prevent the agent from skipping a vital step or proposing a logically impossible action, as the "plan" is essentially a sophisticated guess based on linguistic patterns rather than a validated structural map.

In terms of Task Execution, LLM-based agents typically operate through either direct handling or tool invocation. 
In the former, the LLM processes information and generates a response based on its internal weights; in the latter, the agent must translate its intent into a structured output \citep{Liu_2024}, such as a JSON-formatted function call, to interact with an execution tool. 
The tool returns a raw response, which the LLM must then re-process into a natural language summary or use as a catalyst for the next step in the plan. 
This execution phase is where the "grounding" of the agent is tested, as it requires the model to precisely map its internal reasoning to the strict syntactic requirements of external software. 
Any misalignment between the LLM's generative output and the tool's expected schema results in execution failure, highlighting the fragility of relying on linguistic intuition for technical interoperability.



Despite the sophistication of these components, single-agent systems frequently succumb to catastrophic failure when applied to complex, multi-stage enterprise workflows. These failures are primarily attributed to the "unconstrained" nature of prompt-based planning \citep{rana2025modelfirstreasoningllmagents}. Without an external governing structure, a single agent often enters a hallucination recursive loop, where it attempts to correct an initial error by generating further inaccurate logic based on that false premise \citep{latitude2024taskcomplexity,cheng2025chainofthoughtpromptingobscureshallucination}. Additionally, as the task complexity increases, the agent suffers from contextual overload; the accumulation of previous thoughts, tool outputs, and observations clutters the context window, leading to a precipitous degradation in reasoning quality \citep{anthropic2024context}. This architectural fragility underscores the necessity for moving away from isolated, prompt-driven agents toward a more rigid, orchestrated multi-agent environment where the "world model" is held by a Knowledge Graph rather than the volatile and finite memory of a single model.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Systems}
The evolution of agentic architectures from single-model entities to Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) represents a strategic transition toward functional decomposition and collaborative problem-solving. 
In a MAS environment, a complex global objective is partitioned into discrete sub-tasks, each managed by specialized agents possessing distinct roles, personas, and toolsets \citep{yang2024llmbasedmultiagentsystemstechniques}. 
This shift is predicated on the observation that the collective intelligence of a modular system consistently outperforms the reasoning capacity of a monolithic LLM, particularly in tasks requiring high cognitive diversity or parallel execution \citep{li2024more_agents,guo2024largelanguagemodelbased}. By isolating specific domains of expertise such as an agent dedicated solely to code generation and another to quality assurance the system introduces a layer of intersubjective verifiability, where agents can critique and refine each other's outputs, effectively serving as a circuit breaker for the "hallucination recursive loops" prevalent in single-model configurations.

Despite these advantages, the transition to MAS introduces a significant coordination overhead, commonly referred to as the “Coordination Tax” \citep{kim2025sciencescalingagentsystems}, as the number of agents increases, the complexity of managing their interactions grows exponentially, necessitating sophisticated communication protocols and collaboration strategies. 
\citet{wang2025largemodelbasedagents} distinguishes between "Horizontal" collaboration, where agents interact as peers in an unstructured manner, and "Vertical" or "Hierarchical" orchestration, where a lead agent or central controller dictates the workflow. Without a formal governing structure, even these collaborative models remain bound by the limitations of the underlying models' ability to maintain a consistent state across fragmented context windows.

Consequently, \citet{valmeekam2023planbenchextensiblebenchmarkevaluating} underscores that while Large Language Models (LLMs) can generate text that mimics the structure of a plan, they consistently fail at logically validated execution. This empirical "planning gap" demonstrates that agent collaboration and orchestration cannot be left to the probabilistic nature of the LLM alone. By shifting the responsibility of workflow logic from the LLM to a structured world model, the framework ensures that every agentic action is not just linguistically plausible, but mathematically verified and fully auditable. This transition effectively moves the system from a paradigm of "stochastic sequencing" to one of "deterministic coordination," providing the reliability required for enterprise-grade applications.

\subsection{Knowledge Graphs}

The final component in this theoretical foundation is the Knowledge Graph (KG), a multi-relational graph consisting of entities as nodes and their interactions as edges \citep{Hogan_2021}. 
Historically, Knowledge Graphs emerged from the Semantic Web movement and classical symbolic AI, where they were used to provide a structured, machine-readable representation of world knowledge independent of natural language processing. 
Outside LLMs, KGs are utilized for a variety of applications such as expert systems \citep{jiang2025evolutionknowledgegraphssurvey}, recommendation engines \citep{catherine2016personalized}, and data integration \citep{noy2019industry}, providing a deterministic framework for representing facts in a $G = (V, E, R)$ structure, where $V$ is a set of vertices (entities), $R$ is a set of relation types, and $E \subseteq V \times R \times V$ represents the directed, labeled edges or "triples."

\citep{kejriwal2021knowledge} highlights two primary models that dominate the architectural landscape: Resource Description Framework (RDF) and Labeled Property Graphs (LPG). 
RDF is a standardized model for data interchange that treats every statement as a "triple" $(subject, predicate, object)$. It is highly interoperable and often used for web-scale data integration using the SPARQL query language. 
Conversely, Property Graphs allow for a more flexible, internal data structure where both nodes and edges can contain "properties" (key-value pairs). While RDF is rooted in formal logic and global URI standards, Property Graphs are optimized for high-performance traversal and are frequently used in enterprise settings where complex, interconnected metadata must be queried efficiently using languages like Cypher.

The operational utility of a KG is realized through Graph Databases and their associated Traversal Algorithms. 
Unlike relational databases that rely on expensive "JOIN" operations, graph databases use index-free adjacency, meaning each node effectively points to its neighbors \citep{stegeman2023native}. 
This allows for complex pathfinding and semantic traversal, which can be mathematically expressed as finding a sequence of nodes $v_0, v_1, \dots, v_n$ such that $(v_i, r, v_{i+1}) \in E$ for each $i$. This capability is crucial for identifying functional dependencies; if a goal state $S_g$ is reachable from a current state $S_c$, a graph traversal algorithm can determine the optimal sequence of agentic transitions $a_1, a_2, \dots, a_k$ required to bridge the gap, ensuring that each step satisfies the pre-conditions defined in the graph.

The integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) and Knowledge Graphs (KGs) has evolved into a bidirectional synergy designed to address the inherent limitations of both technologies, primarily by bridging the gap between probabilistic linguistic intuition and deterministic symbolic logic. 
As highlighted in foundational surveys \citet{Pan_2024,kau2024combiningknowledgegraphslarge}, this intersection is typically categorized into three paradigms: KG-enhanced LLMs, where structured facts ground generative outputs to mitigate hallucinations; LLM-enhanced KGs, where models automate the labor-intensive tasks of entity extraction and relationship prediction; and Synergized LLM + KG frameworks, where both components interact in a continuous neuro-symbolic feedback loop. 
Practical applications of these paradigms have demonstrated significant improvements in system reliability; for instance, KG-RAG \citep{sanmartin2024kgragbridginggapknowledge} utilizes the topological structure of a graph to perform multi-hop reasoning that exceeds the capabilities of standard vector-based retrieval, 
while further research explores the use of KGs to refine tool and agent retrieval mechanisms \citep{nizar2025agentasagraphknowledgegraphbasedtool}, ensuring that the selection of external functions is grounded in a structured understanding of their functional dependencies and semantic constraints. 
Specialized techniques continue to refine this integration, such as the "Chain-of-Knowledge" (CoK) framework \citep{zhang2024chainofknowledgeintegratingknowledgereasoning}, which allows LLMs to learn structured reasoning paths directly from KGs to internalize symbolic logic, and the "Self-Refinement-Enhanced Knowledge Retrieval" approach, which introduces an iterative loop where the LLM critiques its own retrieved facts against a KG to eliminate contradictions \citep{niu2024mitigatinghallucinationslargelanguage}. 
Additionally, advancements in "Question-guided KG Re-scoring and Injection" \citep{zhang-etal-2024-question} optimize Question Answering (KGQA) by dynamically re-weighting the relevance of graph nodes based on the specific linguistic context of the query, collectively highlighting a shift from passive data retrieval toward active, structural reasoning where the Knowledge Graph serves as the definitive logical anchor for generative AI.

\section{Review of Related Works}
This section reviews the evolution of Multi-Agent Collaboration, focusing on the transition from heuristic-driven interactions to formal Structured Orchestration Mechanisms. It narrows the analysis to Dynamic Structured Orchestration, where adaptive coordination is achieved in complex, non-linear environments. These works highlight the "planning gap" in current systems and the necessity for the factually grounded architectures proposed in this framework.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Orchestration}
Let's begin by adopting multi-agent system formalism  from \citet{guo2024largelanguagemodelbased,zhu2025agentic}.
An agentic system $\mathcal{S}$ is defined as a four-tuple $\mathcal{S} = \langle \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{C}, \Omega \rangle$, where $\mathcal{A} = \{a_1, \dots, a_n\}$ is a set of $n \ge 1$ agents, $\mathcal{E}$ is the shared environment, $\mathcal{C}$ is the communication topology, and $\Omega$ is the orchestration policy. Systems where $|\mathcal{A}|=1$ are Single-Agent Systems (SAS), while $|\mathcal{A}|>1$ are Multi-Agent Systems (MAS).
Each agent $a_i \in \mathcal{A}$ is a tuple $a_i = \langle \Phi_i, \mathcal{X}_i, \mathcal{M}_i, \pi_i \rangle$, consisting of a reasoning policy $\Phi_i$ (typically an LLM), an action space $\mathcal{X}_i = \{ \text{ToolCall}(t, \theta) : t \in \mathcal{T}, \theta \in \Theta_t \}$, internal memory $\mathcal{M}_i$, and a decision function $\pi_i : \mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{X}_i$ mapping history $\mathcal{H}$ to actions.
At timestep $t$, the execution logic follows:
$$\alpha_{i,t} = \pi_i(h_{i,t})$$
$$o_{i,t} = \mathcal{E}(\alpha_{i,t})$$
$$h_{i,t+1} = h_{i,t} \oplus (\alpha_{i,t}, o_{i,t})$$
The history $h_{i,t}$ is initialized as $\{s_0\}$ and updated iteratively, subject to a maximum context constraint $|h_{i,t+1}| \le K$. Communication in MAS is facilitated by $\Omega$ through message passing between agents.

To categorize the structural variations in multi-agent coordination, \citet{kim2025sciencescalingagentsystems} establishes a formal distinction between communication (the mechanical passing of messages) and coordination (the strategic direction of agent activities). 
This distinction is operationalized through the definition of a communication topology $C$, which governs the information flow patterns within an agent system $\mathcal{S}$. 
The paper identifies four primary topological configurations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Independent} ($C = \emptyset$): Characterized by a total absence of inter-agent communication, where agents operate in isolation.
    
    \item \textbf{Centralized} ($C = \{(a_{\text{orch}}, a_i) : \forall i\}$): Information flow is strictly restricted to an orchestrator-to-agent star pattern.
    
    \item \textbf{Decentralized} ($C = \{(a_i, a_j) : \forall i, j, i \neq j\}$): An all-to-all topology allowing for peer-to-peer information fusion.
    
    \item \textbf{Hybrid} ($C = C_{\text{centralized}} \cup C_{\text{peer}}$): A composite structure that combines hierarchical oversight with limited lateral peer-to-peer exchange.
\end{itemize}
These topologies lead to four specific MAS Configurations, each presenting unique trade-offs between parallelization, coordination depth, and computational overhead. An Independent MAS ($A = \{a_1, \dots, a_n\}, \Omega = synthesis\_only$) maximizes parallelization but offers minimal coordination, making it suitable for ensemble-style reasoning with a complexity of $O(nk)$. 
Conversely, a Centralized MAS ($A = \{a_{orch}, a_1, \dots, a_n\}, \Omega = hierarchical$) stabilizes reasoning through hierarchical verification but introduces a central bottleneck, scaling at $O(rnk)$ across $r$ coordination rounds. 
In a Decentralized MAS ($A = \{a_1, \dots, a_n\}, \Omega = consensus$), agents engage in $d$ sequential debate rounds to reach a collective agreement, incurring a memory complexity of $O(dnk)$ as each agent maintains its own debate history. 
Finally, the Hybrid MAS ($\Omega = hierarchical + lateral$) attempts to inherit the control of a central orchestrator while maintaining the flexibility of lateral peer exchanges, represented by the complexity $O(rnk + pn)$. 
By formalizing these architectures, the research enables a structural ablation of coordination mechanics, allowing performance gains to be attributed to specific topological designs rather than generic multi-agent effects.

\citet{tran2025multiagentcollaborationmechanismssurvey} also identifies a multi-dimensional taxonomy consisting of Collaboration Types (Cooperation, Competition, Coopetition), Strategies (Rule-based, Role-based, Model-based),Communication Structures (Centralized, Decentralized, Hierarchical) and Coordination and Orchestration (Static , Dynamic Architectures). 
However, the primary focus of this project is on Coordination and Orchestration, which is defined as how collaboration channels are created, ordered, and characterized, effectively forming the backbone of multi-agent interactions.

\textbf{Static architectures} rely on domain-specific knowledge and predefined rules to establish fixed collaboration channels. This approach ensures that interactions align with rigid requirements, often leveraging sequential connections (chaining) where the output of one agent ($y_i$) feeds into the next ($y_{i+1}$) alongside the initial input ($x_{collab}$):
$$y_{i+1} = y_i \parallel x_i \parallel x_{collab}$$
By utilizing fixed workflows, systems like MapCoder \citep{islam2024mapcodermultiagentcodegeneration} (program synthesis), MACRec \citep{Wang_2024} (recommendations), and specialized Literary Translation frameworks like \citet{wu2025perhapshumantranslationharnessing} can emulate professional human pipelines. While highly effective for predictable tasks involving recall, planning, and debugging, these architectures lack the flexibility to adapt to novel or evolving task requirements outside their predefined schema.

In contrast, \textbf{Dynamic Architectures} are designed for high adaptability, utilizing Management Agents or adaptive mechanisms to assign roles and define collaboration channels in real-time. This allows the system to respond to the unique nuances of each input rather than following a static script. 
A prominent implementation of this flexibility is the graph-based orchestration mechanism that an Orchestrator agent dynamically constructs a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) where nodes represent discrete tasks and edges define their functional dependencies \citep{gabriel2024advancingagenticsystemsdynamic}. This structure enables the system to execute sub-tasks in a optimized parallel or sequential order as dictated by the input. By employing a Delegator agent to consolidate results from all completed nodes, dynamic architectures significantly enhance responsiveness and scalability for complex, multi-step queries that require a non-linear execution sequence.

The analysis of these paradigms reveals that there is no single, unifying definition or classification for Multi-Agent Systems (MAS). Instead, a system’s identity is fluid, often exhibiting multiple characteristics simultaneously based on specific architectural requirements. By mapping popular frameworks to these theoretical models, we can better understand how theoretical "backbones" manifest in practical engineering.

The fluidity of these classifications is best exemplified by AutoGen's Group Chat pattern \citep{autogen_groupchat_pattern}, which is cooperative and role-based but fluctuates between centralized and decentralized structures depending on the presence of a manager agent. 
While \citet{kim2025sciencescalingagentsystems} would classify this as a Hybrid topology, its orchestration remains inherently Dynamic as turn-taking is determined in real-time.

\subsection{Shift Towards Structured But Dynamic Orchestration}
This paradigm bridges the gap between deterministic control and agentic flexibility by employing explicitly defined control flows that remain highly adaptable rather than following a static, linear script. By hybridizing structured logic with real-time decision-making, these frameworks ensure that multi-agent interactions are both architecturally grounded and responsive to evolving task requirements.
To explore the shift toward structured yet dynamic orchestration, we analyze how recent frameworks transition from static chains to complex, topology-aware coordination. These works move beyond simple message passing, instead treating the multi-agent interaction space as a programmable and adaptable graph.

 The \textbf{Graph of Agents (GoA)} framework \citep{joo2025graphagentsprincipledlong}, for instance, operates through a layered information fusion architecture where a query is decomposed and processed in parallel by an initial tier of agents. These preliminary outputs are then funneled into subsequent layers of aggregator agents that resolve contradictions and synthesize a unified response. The mechanical core of GoA is its topology-aware scoring system, which assigns dynamic weights to each agent’s contribution during the fusion process, ensuring that the most rational and contextually relevant reasoning paths are prioritized in the final output.

Advancing this adaptability, \textbf{DyTopo} \citep{lu2026dytopodynamictopologyrouting} introduces a highly specialized semantic routing mechanism that reconfigures the communication network for every unique task. Unlike frameworks with fixed edges, DyTopo treats the multi-agent system as a latent pool of capabilities, employing a Topology Manager to analyze the semantic intent of a query and predict the optimal functional dependencies between agents. By calculating the semantic similarity and task-relevance of potential agent pairings, the system dynamically activates or prunes communication edges in real-time. This precision routing eliminates the "noise" and redundant token usage typical of all-to-all broadcast models, ensuring that information only flows through edges that provide measurable utility to the sub-task at hand.

In the context of large-scale coordination, \textbf{MacNet} \citep{qian2025scalinglargelanguagemodelbased} organizes agents into specialized topological neighborhoods within a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). This framework utilizes a structural routing protocol that directs the flow of state information based on the hierarchical and functional position of each agent in the network. A key technical feature of MacNet is its use of topological persistence, where the system records and optimizes high-performing routing paths across the DAG over time, effectively allowing the architecture to "learn" more efficient coordination patterns for recurring problem types. This bridges the gap between the rigid constraints of a central orchestrator and the chaotic overhead of purely decentralized debate.



Finally, most recently \citet{benkhaled2026g2cp} introduces \textbf{G2CP (Graph To Communication Protocol)} framework functions as an orchestration compiler that separates the high-level task logic from the execution-level interaction rules. It begins by generating a graph-based plan of the user’s goal and subsequently translates that graph into a formal communication protocol a deterministic set of "rules of engagement" that define the specific states, turn-taking logic, and data schemas required for the agents to collaborate. By decoupling the "what" (the graph) from the "how" (the protocol), G2CP allows for a hybrid state where the global objective is structurally anchored, but the local agent interactions remain fluid and adaptive to real-time feedback. Collectively, these advancements highlight a move toward topology-aware intelligence, where the programmable geometry of the agent network becomes as critical to success as the underlying language models.


\subsection{Extending KG Driven Discovery to MAS}
SAP an Enterprise application software company utilizes Knowledge Graphs (KGs) for API discovery by enriching raw API specifications with semantic metadata and business-process context, moving orchestration beyond the limitations of hard-coded scripts \citep{deeplearningai_kg_api_discovery}. This implementation demonstrates that KGs serve as a foundational "source of truth" for the dynamic, deterministic sequencing of complex tasks. By traversing the ontological relationships within the graph, an orchestrator can perform real-time path-finding to resolve multi-step goals based on verifiable functional dependencies rather than probabilistic generative paths.

This proves that Knowledge Graphs are highly effective for the dynamic sequencing of diverse data types and tasks, ensuring that every transition in a multi-hop workflow is factually grounded and logically valid. Our proposed system seeks to adopt this approach for multi-agent orchestration, leveraging the KG to provide the structural rigor and "Just-in-Time" adaptability required for autonomous, enterprise-grade coordination.

\section{Conclusion}
The analysis of current literature reveals a critical trade-off between architectural rigor and operational flexibility. While graph-based frameworks like MacNet and Graph of Agents (GoA) provide necessary structural constraints through DAGs and layered topologies, their primary gap lies in a tendency to rely on predefined sequencing for specific workflows. By requiring the explicit mapping of node transitions or hierarchical tiers, these systems can become brittle or lose their truly "dynamic" nature when faced with tasks that deviate from their established schema. Similarly, even in more adaptive protocols like G2CP and DyTopo, the logic used to traverse these structures often fluctuates between rigid hard-coding and probabilistic inference.

Future study must address the integration of deterministic symbolic grounding that allows for real-time adaptability without requiring exhaustive manual mapping for every possible scenario. By adopting a Knowledge Graph-based orchestration framework, the proposed system treats the KG as a fluid "source of truth" rather than a fixed flowchart. In this paradigm, the Knowledge Graph serves as an ontological map where nodes represent agentic capabilities and edges define their formal functional dependencies. 
This shift allows the orchestrator to dynamically query the KG to construct valid, context-specific execution paths on the fly. This ensures that the system's "backbone" remains factually grounded in a verifiable symbolic structure, providing the architectural rigor of a DAG with the unscripted flexibility required for complex, autonomous coordination.
