\chapter{LITERATURE REVIEW} % Main chapter title


The evolution of Large Language Models (LLMs) has transcended the boundaries of simple linguistic synthesis, moving toward the realization of agentic systems capable of autonomous, multi-step task execution. While the generative prowess of these models is undeniable, their deployment as singular autonomous entities has exposed significant intrinsic limitations, most notably the propensity for hallucination and the constraints of their autoregressive nature \citep{tran2025multiagentcollaborationmechanismssurvey}. These models often struggle with "slow-thinking" processes \citep{hagendorff2023human} the systematic, deliberative reasoning required for complex problem-solving and face diminishing returns as scaling laws meet the reality of open-ended computational logic \citep{kaplan2020scaling}. To bridge these gaps, most implementations of agentic applications have pivoted toward Multi-Agent Systems (MAS), under the observation that a collective of specialized agents consistently outperforms individual models by distributing cognitive load and introducing cross-verification \citep{albada2025chapter}. This shift, however, necessitates a rigorous definition of how these agents collaborate, communicate, and are orchestrated to ensure that the collective output remains coherent and goal-oriented.

\cite{tran2025multiagentcollaborationmechanismssurvey} describes orchestration in the context of MAS as a multifaceted discipline encompassing collaboration types, coordination strategies, and specific communication structures designed to manage the flow of information between disparate agents. A primary focus within this field is the "sequencing" of agent interventions, which dictates the operational logic of the workflow. In many current frameworks, this sequencing is either statically defined through manual scripting or relies on heuristic-based "context hand-offs," where the state of a task is passed linearly from one agent to the next based on probabilistic triggers. 
Such strategies, while functional for experimental or simple workflows, frequently fail to meet the reliability benchmarks required of enterprise systems, where reliability is defined by the consistency and accuracy of generated outputs \citep{menshawy2025llms}.
Because enterprise deployments demand that reliability be maintained through rigorous testing, validation phases, and robust version control, the inherent "stochastic guessing" of probabilistic sequencing introduces unacceptable inaccuracies. The reliance on models that may be compromised by training biases or learning anomalies necessitates a move toward orchestration that does not just "predict" the next step, but validates it against a quality assurance framework ensuring that every agentic transition is as dependable as a traditional enterprise software deployment.

To anchor these agentic decisions in reality, researchers have historically looked to Knowledge Graphs (KGs) as a robust mechanism for grounding LLM outputs \citep{kau2024combiningknowledgegraphslarge}. Knowledge Graphs provide a structured, symbolic representation of a domain, offering a "Source of Truth" and a relational depth that probabilistic models inherently lack \citep{Hogan_2021}. By integrating KGs into the decision-making loop, systems can verify agent claims against a persistent, factual schema, thereby mitigating the risks of stochastic drift and ensuring semantic consistency. The intersection of graph-based reasoning and agent orchestration represents a significant frontier in AI research, as it promises to transform the way agents understand their environment and their own functional capabilities. This historical precedent for KG-driven grounding suggests that the next logical step in the evolution of MAS is the use of graph structures not just for static data retrieval, but as the fundamental logic gate for orchestration itself.

This chapter provides a comprehensive examination of existing approaches to multi-agent orchestration, tracing the trajectory from general collaboration frameworks to specialized strategies focused on agent sequencing and context hand-off, as well as emerging approaches that propose dynamic methods for managing these transitions. By critically analyzing these methodologies, the review highlights a persistent limitation: the lack of a truly dynamic and deterministic way to decide the order of execution in complex, non-linear workflows. Many current "dynamic" models still operate within the vacuum of the LLM’s context window, leaving the sequencing vulnerable to the same autoregressive failures the MAS architecture was intended to solve. Through this analysis, we establish the critical need for a fact-grounded approach to dynamic agent sequencing. This leads directly to the core contribution of this project: a Knowledge Graph-Based Multi-Agent Orchestration Framework that utilizes a deterministic world model to ensure that every agentic transition is logically validated and anchored in the objective reality of the graph.
